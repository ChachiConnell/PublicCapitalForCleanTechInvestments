The land mismatch — infrastructure need vs capital allocation
Of all renewable energy infrastructure required to reach net zero, 43–50% will be built on First Nations land. Yet First Nations communities receive approximately 0.2% of total public clean energy capital directly. This is both a justice gap and an economic one — the transition cannot be fully delivered without First Nations participation.

Note on scope
These figures capture directly First Nations-designated funding. Additional capital flows to projects on First Nations land via mainstream programs (CEFC, ARENA, NRF, State funds) but without dedicated First Nations outcomes — that data is not publicly disaggregated.
The structural barrier — CEFC mandate gap
Why most First Nations-led projects cannot access the largest clean energy finance vehicle in Australia.
CEFC MANDATE GAP
The CEFC's mandate requires commercial returns. Most First Nations-led projects involve community governance, Native Title land tenure and social outcomes that don't fit commercial underwriting criteria. The exclusion is structural, not intentional.
WHAT'S CHANGING
The First Nations Clean Energy Network's reform paper has generated cautious support from CEFC executive and Ministerial advisers. A First Nations set-aside tender under the Capacity Investment Scheme is planned for 2026. Four CIS projects are currently negotiating equity with First Nations groups.
THE ECONOMIC CASE
43% of required clean energy infrastructure sits on First Nations land. Yindjibarndi Energy Corporation is targeting 3GW in the Pilbara. Ngarluma is planning 5GW. This pipeline is not accessible to mainstream investors without First Nations partnerships — fixing the structural gap unlocks a new and substantial investment frontier.
Capacity Investment Scheme — First Nations progress
CIS is the $70B revenue underwriting scheme for wind, solar and storage. First Nations integration is improving but remains early-stage.
