🌿 First Nations Clean Energy — Capital, Country & the Accountability Gap
First Nations peoples have cared for Country for 65,000 years. Nearly half of Australia's future clean energy infrastructure will be built on that Country. Yet First Nations communities receive less than 1% of the public capital driving this transition — and the CEFC has made $24.2B in commitments since 2012 with zero systematic First Nations equity participation. This is the defining justice and economic failure of Australia's clean energy transition.

The core mismatch: 43% of the renewable energy infrastructure Australia needs to reach net zero will be sited on recognised First Nations land — requiring over $20.9B in capital investment and generating an estimated 700,000 direct jobs. Yet the CEFC — Australia's primary clean energy finance institution — has deployed $24.2B since 2012 with zero operational projects including First Nations equity participation. This is not policy intent. It is structural exclusion: a mandate designed for commercial returns that simply does not accommodate First Nations project structures, land tenure or community benefit models. Sources: CEFC / Clean Energy Council / KPMG / EY Net Zero Centre 2025.

NSW's 121 Aboriginal Land Councils manage 447 sq km of land with a further 7,438 sq km under claim — and the entire current estate is technically suitable for solar development. The report found that strategic support from CEFC and ARENA for land council-developer partnerships could unlock more than 11GW of solar and 1.6GW of wind — yet 70% of land claims lodged over 40 years (covering ~1.12 million hectares) remain undetermined. This is not a lack of resource. It is a lack of policy will and structural finance support.

⚠️ The structural exclusion: how the CEFC mandate locks First Nations out
Source: First Nations Clean Energy Network discussion paper · CEFC executive and Ministerial adviser engagement 2025–26

The CEFC's exclusion of First Nations communities is not explicit policy — it is structural consequence. The CEFC mandate is designed for commercial returns. First Nations projects typically involve community title land (which cannot be used as security), community benefit models that don't generate standard financial returns, and governance structures that don't fit conventional lending criteria. The result: $24.2B committed, 420+ transactions, zero systematic First Nations equity participation.
